SolarEX photovoltaic nanocoatings

Two nanocoating pathways for cleaner solar glass and better field economics.

SolarEX is structured around two mechanism-led technologies. SolarEX Quartz uses SiO₂ to create a transparent hydrophobic and oleophobic barrier that lowers contaminant adhesion and supports easier rinsing. SolarEX Titan uses TiO₂ to introduce photocatalytic organic breakdown and superhydrophilic water-sheeting under light exposure. Together they form a practical framework for matching fouling profile, site climate, and commercial logic.

Mechanism-first selection improves commercial credibility

SolarEX should not be positioned as a generic add-on. The correct recommendation depends on the dominant fouling mechanism, cleaning burden, and local revenue model. Quartz is strongest where dust, mineral spotting, and low-wash operations dominate. Titan is strongest where organic contamination, photochemical cleaning potential, and more uniform rain-assisted rinsing matter most.

SolarEX Quartz

Passive self-cleaning via low surface adhesion

Quartz forms an ultrathin 100–150 nm transparent SiO₂ layer that supports hydrophobic and oleophobic behavior, lowers contaminant adhesion, and helps rainfall or rinse water remove fouling more effectively.

  • Positioned for dust, sand, and wash-reduction logic
  • Up to 70% fewer wash cycles framed in source material
  • 147-day payback framing in Europe and 218-day framing in Middle East scenarios

SolarEX Titan

Active self-cleaning through TiO₂ photocatalysis

Titan uses TiO₂ to decompose organic fouling under UV exposure and create superhydrophilic water-sheeting after activation. The route is supported by a 360-day monitored German rooftop study comparing 63 coated modules against 315 controls.

  • Best fit for pollen, algae, bird fouling, and other organic films
  • 5.15% average measured gain in the monitored study
  • 87-day payback framing in high-insolation scenarios

How the SolarEX business case should be read

The strongest investment logic combines yield uplift with operations-and-maintenance savings. A site-level assessment should therefore track treated-versus-control output, wash frequency, water use, labor load, and cleanliness retention between maintenance cycles.

Technical qualification

  • Dominant fouling type and site climate
  • Panel age, tilt, and cleaning history
  • Rainfall profile and exposure to dust, pollen, or biofilm drivers

Commercial qualification

  • Energy price or tariff structure
  • Current wash cost, water use, and labor burden
  • Downtime or access cost linked to cleaning events
