Application process

Field workflow from pilot design to curing and post-application validation.

SolarEX application does not require a complicated installation ecosystem, but it does require process discipline. The difference between a persuasive pilot and an inconclusive one is usually found in surface preparation, control logic, environmental conditions during application, and proper post-treatment monitoring.

Application sequence

1. Define the pilot geometry

Select treated and untreated control areas with comparable operating conditions and clear measurement boundaries.

2. Prepare the surface

Clean thoroughly, remove oils, residues, surfactants, and incompatible prior treatments, and ensure the surface is dry and stable.

3. Apply uniformly

Use spray or wipe application within the documented coverage range and maintain even wetness across the full target area.

4. Cure and protect

Allow initial drying and full cure before exposing the treated surface to moisture, wash events, or aggressive field disturbance.

Curing and immediate post-application management

Public SolarEX material frames roughly 30 seconds to tack dry and approximately 24 hours to full cure for both Quartz and Titan. Weather, panel temperature, humidity, and formulation choice can affect the practical field window, so application timing remains an important operational variable.

Before application

  • Document baseline output and recent cleaning history.
  • Capture site photography for treated and control zones.
  • Confirm electrical safety, ventilation, and weather window.

During application

  • De-energize where required by site safety rules.
  • Use correct PPE and ignition controls for solvent-based handling.
  • Check for misses, runs, and inconsistent application density.

After application

  • Track rainfall, wash events, and notable fouling episodes.
  • Monitor output and compare treated versus control behavior.
  • Translate results into both technical and commercial outcomes.

Why application discipline matters commercially

A good pilot does more than show a clean panel. It produces evidence that can survive technical review: output separation, photographic cleanliness retention, clear wash-event history, and a cost model built around real site conditions. That is what moves a buyer from curiosity to deployment planning.
